A digital museum experience dedicated to the history, evolution, and cultural impact of pickleball — one of the fastest-growing sports in America.

## Museum Topic

Courts of Time explores the remarkable journey of pickleball — from a backyard invention in 1965 to a nationwide cultural phenomenon. The museum guides visitors through five exhibits covering the sport's origins, its timeline of growth, the surge in mainstream popularity, a visual gallery, and its lasting legacy.

The central story is simple but compelling: a game invented by three fathers to entertain their bored kids became the fastest-growing sport in America. That journey — from backyard to arena — is the emotional and narrative backbone of the entire experience.

## Design Style — Editorial Modernism

The site uses an Editorial Modernist design style, characterized by:

- Large, bold typography that commands attention
- A dark navy background with high-contrast orange accents
- Full-screen dramatic hero sections
- Clean, structured layouts with clear visual hierarchy
- Purposeful use of white space to let content breathe

This style was chosen because it gives the subject matter weight and credibility. Pickleball may seem like a casual topic, but editorial modernism treats it with the same seriousness as any cultural or historical subject — which is exactly the tone a museum demands.

## Brand Archetype — The Explorer

The site is built around The Explorer archetype.

The Explorer invites discovery. It speaks to visitors who want to uncover something new, go on a journey, and come back changed. Every element of Courts of Time is designed to pull the visitor forward — from the entry point ("Discover the remarkable journey...") through five consecutive exhibits that build on each other.

The Explorer archetype shaped:
- The tone — curious, adventurous, forward-moving
- The vocabulary — "journey," "discover," "unforgettable"
- The structure — a guided route that rewards exploration
- The emotional experience — visitors feel like they are uncovering something real

## Cialdini Principle — Social Proof

The primary Cialdini principle used is Social Proof.

Social proof works by signaling that many others have already validated something — making the visitor more likely to trust and engage with it.

In Courts of Time, social proof appears through:
- "The fastest-growing sport on Earth" — immediately establishes mass adoption
- "5 exhibits · 7 milestones · One unforgettable story" — signals depth and credibility
- The Gallery exhibit — showing real people playing reinforces that this is a real cultural movement

When visitors see that millions of people have embraced pickleball, they become more curious and more willing to invest time in the experience.

## AI Orchestration Process

AI was used intentionally and strategically throughout every stage of this project — not as a shortcut, but as a tool under human direction.

### Research Stage
Used Claude AI to research the history of pickleball, identify key milestones, understand the sport's cultural significance, and determine what narrative structure would work best for a museum format.

### Planning Stage
Used AI to help plan the five-exhibit structure, determine the main story arc (backyard invention → cultural phenomenon), and decide what content belonged in each exhibit vs. what was supporting material.

### Design Stage
Used Lovable (AI-powered development platform) to build the React-based site. Made deliberate design decisions about typography, color palette, layout, and visual hierarchy. Directed the AI toward an editorial modernist aesthetic with dark navy and orange accents.

### Content Stage
Used AI to draft and refine the written content for each exhibit, then reviewed and edited every section for accuracy, tone, and clarity. AI output was always reviewed and revised — never used without judgment.

### Revision Stage
Reviewed the site multiple times, identified weak areas, and directed AI to improve specific sections. Made final decisions on what stayed, what changed, and what was removed.

## How the Site Was Planned, Built, Reviewed, and Improved

**Planning:** Started by identifying the topic (pickleball history), researching the major turning points, and mapping out a five-exhibit narrative structure. Decided the experience should feel like a guided tour — visitors should always know where they are and where they are going next.

**Building:** Used Lovable to generate the initial site structure, then iteratively improved each section. Applied a consistent design system across all pages — same typography, color palette, spacing, and visual language throughout.

**Reviewing:** Walked through the full visitor experience multiple times, checking for narrative clarity, visual consistency, and ease of navigation. Identified gaps in content and sections that needed stronger writing or better visual support.

**Improving:** Refined the homepage hero to immediately communicate the main story. Strengthened exhibit transitions so visitors feel guided rather than lost. Improved the visual gallery to better support the social proof narrative. Polished typography and spacing for a more professional, museum-quality result.
